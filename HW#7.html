df_unique = df.groupby('League Name', as_index=False)['Viewership'].mean()


labels = df_unique['League Name']
values = df_unique['Viewership']


order = np.argsort(values)
y = np.arange(len(labels))


fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10,6))
ax.barh(y, values[order], color='royalblue')


for i, v in enumerate(values[order]):
    ax.text(v + 10, i, f"{v:.0f}M", va='center', fontsize=9)


ax.set_yticks(y)
ax.set_yticklabels(np.array(labels)[order])
ax.set_xlabel('Average Viewership')
ax.set_title('Top Leagues by Viewership')


ax.annotate('Highest-value league',
            xy=(values.max(), np.argmax(values)),
            xytext=(values.max() + 100, np.argmax(values) - 0.3),
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle='->', lw=1.2))
ax.annotate('Smaller cluster below',
            xy=(values.min(), np.argmin(values)),
            xytext=(values.min() + 150, np.argmin(values) + 0.5),
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle='->', lw=1.2))

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Reflection

For this chart, I wanted to make it easy to see how the different leagues compare in average viewership. I chose a horizontal bar chart because it gives plenty of space for the league names on the y-axis without crowding, which can happen with vertical bars. Sorting the bars from lowest to highest viewership helps the eye follow the progression and quickly spot which leagues have the most viewers.

I added labels directly on the bars showing the viewership in millions so you don’t have to guess from the axis. The y-axis labels match the sorted order, so each bar lines up with its league name. The x-axis is labeled clearly as “Average Viewership,” and the title makes it obvious what the chart is about.

I also added a couple of annotations to point out interesting details. One highlights the league with the highest viewership, and the other points out the smaller group of leagues with lower numbers. This helps guide attention to patterns that might otherwise be overlooked. The bars are all the same color—royal blue—so the focus stays on the data rather than flashy colors.

The table also was organized in order of the average viewship leagues and all other metrics from the original data were taken out.

Overall, these choices were all about making the chart readable, straightforward, and visually clean, while also pointing out the key takeaways so anyone can quickly understand which leagues are leading in viewership.
